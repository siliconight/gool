# Saturation v2 — ADAA + multi-mode shaper + tilt

**Status:** Design proposal. Targets v0.30.0 (post-v0.29.2). Replaces the single-shape `tanh` waveshaper currently in `src/audio_engine/dsp/saturation_effect.{h,cpp}`. Companion to `reverb_dattorro.md` — same "do the upgrade the same way we did reverb" approach: keep the param ID range stable, soft-migrate JSON keys, add new params at the end of the EffectParameter enum.

**Design target:** Light-to-medium saturation for game-audio bus glue, transient reinforcement, and "in-world distortion" effects (radio comms, damaged speakers). NOT a guitar amp simulator, NOT a high-gain tube emulator. The constraint Pirkle calls out for "clean tube" usage applies: stay in the linear-with-a-bend region, do not try to model grid-conduction or cascaded overdrive.

---

## 1. Why the current saturation is the weak link

The existing implementation is a single-stage `tanh((x + bias) * drive)` waveshaper with parallel dry/wet mix. It does one thing — adds soft odd-harmonics with optional asymmetry — and it does it adequately. Three concrete acoustic limitations:

### 1.1 Aliasing on bright sources

`tanh` is a non-bandlimited nonlinearity. At drive ≥ 2.5 on transient-rich material (gunshot clicks, footsteps on grit, fricatives in dialogue, cymbal hits in music) the upper harmonics generated by the shaper exceed Nyquist and fold back as aliasing. The current header file acknowledges this and recommends "do aggressive distortion offline." That recommendation is right for the current implementation, and it is also the thing this redesign removes the need for.

The textbook fix is 2× or 4× polyphase oversampling around the shaper. A cheaper and arguably better fix has become standard since 2016: **Antiderivative Anti-Aliasing (ADAA)** [Parker, Zavalishin, Le Bivic, DAFX 2016]. ADAA is roughly 3-5× the cost of a raw shaper, no allocations, no buffer management, no polyphase filter design. It is the right tool for our cost budget.

### 1.2 One shape, one character

A `tanh` curve has one character: smooth, symmetric, dominant 3rd harmonic, with even harmonics from the `bias` term. That character is good for "console glue" but does not cover:

- **Tube-like soft saturation** (asinh, 3/2-power-law) — gentler shoulder, "warm" 2nd-harmonic emphasis, the sound you reach for on dialogue and music bus glue
- **Tape-like compression** (hyperbolic, soft-quadratic) — gentle level compression with harmonic side-effect, the sound for music bus and impact reinforcement
- **Diode/op-amp clipping** (hard-clip, cubic) — sharp shoulder, dominant odd harmonics, the sound for radio comms, damaged-speaker effects, gunshot bite reinforcement

A single saturation effect block with a `mode` selector gives sound designers four distinct flavors out of one DSP node, at the cost of one extra discrete parameter.

### 1.3 No tone shaping

A single static shape applied to broadband signal saturates every band equally. The classic tube/tape move is to **pre-emphasize** the band you want to saturate hardest, run the shaper, then **de-emphasize** to undo the EQ tilt. The result is saturation that's frequency-dependent without changing the overall tonal balance. One parameter ("tilt" or "tone") makes the saturation reach into highs vs lows. Cheap to implement, audibly transformative.

## 2. Reference review

Four references were consulted for this design:

### 2.1 Hack Audio: Hard Clipping & Soft Clipping
Practical cookbook. The canonical cubic soft-clip formula is `y = x - x³/3` (with `|x| ≤ 1` clamping at the shoulder where the derivative becomes zero). Hard clip is the trivial `clamp(x, -threshold, +threshold)`. Both serve as the discrete-shape references for "edgy" modes; both are the textbook examples of nonlinearities that need ADAA when drive is non-trivial.

### 2.2 Pirkle, Addendum A19: Vacuum Tube & Distortion Emulation Part 2
Tube emulation theory. Key takeaways for our scope:

- **Asymmetric distortion** (positive and negative halves treated differently) is the defining acoustic signature of "tube character." Our current `bias` parameter approximates this but does it via DC offset before tanh, which is mathematically one-sided but tonally weak. A proper asymmetric mode would gain different drive amounts for `x > 0` vs `x < 0`.
- **3/2-power law** for triode plate current produces the soft, even-harmonic-rich character that musicians describe as "strong" or "warm." For our purposes, `asinh(x)` is a close-enough lightweight approximation with the right shoulder shape and unbounded range.
- **Operating-point constraint**: Pirkle explicitly excludes high-gain guitar amp grid-conduction modeling from his Part 1 (the part covered by the addendum's first half). We're firmly in Part 1 territory — preamp-style "clean tube" saturation, not amp-sim.

We do not attempt to model the full tube load-line or implement the ClassAValve and ClassBValvePair objects from the addendum. That's out of scope; the relevant insight for us is **shape variety + asymmetry**, not full circuit emulation.

### 2.3 Faust `misceffects` library, Saturators section
Lists two trivial (non-antialiased) saturators: `softclipQuadratic` (Zölzer) and `wavefold`. Also in the Dynamic section: `cubicnl(drive, offset)` is the cubic soft-clipper from the JOS PASP reference, with the `offset` parameter playing the same role as our current `bias`. This is the canonical reference implementation for a cubic mode in our shape table.

### 2.4 Faust `aanl` (antialiased) library — the primary reference
This is the most important reference for this design. It implements ADAA following Parker/Zavalishin/Le Bivic 2016. The library provides first-order (`ADAA1`) and second-order (`ADAA2`) generalized ADAA wrappers, plus pre-built antialiased versions of every saturator we'd want: `hardclip`, `cubic1`, `parabolic`, `hyperbolic`, `softclipQuadratic1`, `tanh1`, `arctan`, `asinh1`, etc. `tanh` is only available in first-order ADAA — the second-order antiderivative requires the dilogarithm function, not worth implementing for our use case.

Two important caveats from the library documentation that shape our implementation:

1. **Antialiased saturators perform best with high-amplitude input.** At low drive levels (close to 1.0, signal barely clipping), ADAA introduces a small amount of noise from the division-by-difference. The recommended pattern is to crossfade between the trivial shaper and the ADAA shaper based on input amplitude (or, simpler, based on drive setting). We follow this pattern.
2. **Double-precision is vital.** ADAA performs `(F(x[n]) - F(x[n-1])) / (x[n] - x[n-1])`. When `x[n] ≈ x[n-1]` the denominator approaches zero and we need to fall back to a Taylor expansion or to evaluating `f((x[n]+x[n-1])/2)` directly. The threshold for falling back must be tuned against the FP precision used. We use double-precision internally for the ADAA computation (the rest of the engine is float; we'll convert at the boundaries of `Process`).

## 3. Goals and non-goals

### Goals
1. Eliminate audible aliasing for drive up to 4.0 on bright/transient material
2. Provide 4 distinct saturation character modes that cover the game-audio use cases (glue, tube, tape, fuzz)
3. Add a tone-shaping parameter that controls frequency-dependent saturation
4. Maintain 100% bypass cost when `mix == 0` (current behavior; non-negotiable)
5. Keep the param surface to ≤6 parameters, matching the reverb's surface size
6. Preserve backward compatibility: existing `config.json` files load and sound identical
7. Stay stateless or near-stateless — no large buffers, no allocations after `Prepare`, no env followers
8. Add one new EffectKind-style introspection hook (`Mode()`) for the dock to render the right widget

### Non-goals
1. Guitar amp simulation, cascaded multi-stage overdrive, grid-conduction modeling
2. Wave digital filter (WDF) tube emulation
3. Built-in cabinet/speaker IR convolution
4. Sample-and-hold, bitcrushing, ring-modulation (separate effect types if ever needed)
5. Spectral processing, FFT-domain shaping
6. Lookahead, transient detection, envelope-following gain — adds latency and state we don't want for a per-bus glue effect
7. Replacing the existing "shape it in your DAW first" guidance — we're not trying to turn this into a sound design *tool*, just a better in-engine glue/character/bite effect

## 4. Topology

```
                          input
                            │
                            ▼
                ┌───────────────────────┐
                │  pre-emphasis (tilt)  │   ◄── one-pole high-shelf, ±N dB
                └───────────────────────┘       skip entirely when tilt=0
                            │
                            ▼
                ┌───────────────────────┐
                │   × drive_mapped + bias │   pre-shaper gain
                └───────────────────────┘
                            │
                            ▼
                ┌───────────────────────┐
                │      ADAA shaper      │   ◄── f(x) selected by `mode`
                │   (first-order ADAA)  │       crossfades to trivial near low-drive
                └───────────────────────┘
                            │
                            ▼
                ┌───────────────────────┐
                │      DC blocker       │   ◄── 1-pole HPF @ ~20 Hz
                └───────────────────────┘       (replaces current static DC-subtract)
                            │
                            ▼
                ┌───────────────────────┐
                │  auto-compensation    │   ◄── deterministic per-mode/drive table
                └───────────────────────┘       multiplier to keep wet RMS≈dry RMS
                            │
                            ▼
                ┌───────────────────────┐
                │  de-emphasis (tilt)   │   ◄── inverse one-pole shelf
                └───────────────────────┘       (skip when tilt=0)
                            │
                            ▼
                ┌───────────────────────┐
                │  × outputGain (trim)  │   ◄── post-trim, user-adjustable
                └───────────────────────┘
                            │
                            ▼
                ┌───────────────────────┐
                │   mix dry + wet       │   ◄── parallel blend, unchanged
                └───────────────────────┘
                            │
                            ▼
                          output
```

State per channel: one ADAA delay sample (`x[n-1]`), one pre-emphasis filter state (one-pole), one de-emphasis filter state (one-pole), one DC blocker state (one-pole). Four scalar floats per channel; stereo total is 8 floats. No allocations after `Prepare`.

## 5. Parameter surface (6 params, matches reverb)

| ID | EffectParameter | JSON key | Range | Default | Notes |
|----|-----------------|----------|-------|---------|-------|
| 17 | `Saturation_Drive` | `drive` | 0..1 | 0.0 | normalized, mapped per mode (see §6) — was 0..N, soft-migrate |
| 18 | `Saturation_Mix` | `mix` | 0..1 | 0.0 | parallel blend, unchanged |
| 19 | `Saturation_OutputGain` | `output_gain` | 0..2 | 1.0 | post-trim only; auto-comp is always on internally |
| 20 | `Saturation_Bias` | `bias` | -1..1 | 0.0 | asymmetry; semantics change slightly per mode |
| 26 | `Saturation_Mode` | `mode` | discrete 0..3 | 0 (`Tanh`) | new — see §6 |
| 27 | `Saturation_Tone` | `tone` | -1..1 | 0.0 | new — pre/de-emphasis tilt, 0 = bypass filter |

Param ID 26 is the first free slot after the v0.29.2 reverb additions (which consume 23, 24, 25). Param ID 27 immediately follows. No conflicts with the reverb's alias retention for old `Reverb_RoomSize` (9) and `Reverb_Damping` (10).

### 5.1 Soft migration

Same pattern as v0.29.0 reverb. Two compatibility concerns:

- **`drive` was previously unnormalized** (typical range 1.5..4.0). Now it's normalized 0..1 mapped to a useful range per mode (see §6). On `bus_config_loader.cpp` load, if we see a `drive` value > 1.0, infer this is legacy unnormalized data and remap: `new_drive = (legacy_drive - 1.0) / (4.0 - 1.0)`, clamped to 0..1. Log once.
- **`output_gain` was previously > 0 unbounded.** Now it's 0..2 (a trim, not a makeup). Existing values that exceeded 2.0 were likely manual makeup for high drive — those users will need to re-set output gain post-load. Log once per offending bus.

Existing `config.json` files with just `drive=1.0`, `mix=0.0` (the default-off case) load identically with zero perceptual change.

## 6. Shape modes

Four modes, each with a different acoustic character and a different drive mapping curve. Selection is via the `mode` parameter (discrete int).

### 6.1 Mode table

| `mode` | Name | Shape function | Drive map (0..1 → input scale) | Character | Use cases |
|--------|------|----------------|--------------------------------|-----------|-----------|
| 0 | `Tanh` | `tanh(x)` | 1.0..4.0 | Symmetric soft, odd-harmonic dominant, smooth shoulder | Console glue, music bus, *default — matches existing v0.29.x sound at drive≈1.0..2.5* |
| 1 | `Tube` | `asinh(x) / asinh(1)` | 1.0..3.0 | Unbounded, gentle shoulder, easy 2nd-harmonic via bias | Dialogue glue, music bus warmth |
| 2 | `Tape` | `softQuadratic(x)` | 1.0..3.0 | Bounded ±1, gentle level compression, even/odd mix | Impact reinforcement, music bus glue |
| 3 | `Diode` | `cubic(x) = x - x³/3` clamped | 1.0..6.0 | Bounded ±2/3, sharp shoulder above threshold, odd-dominant | Radio comms, broken-speaker FX, gunshot bite |

Where `softQuadratic(x)` is the Zölzer formulation: `sign(x) * (1 - (1 - |x|)²)` for `|x| ≤ 1`, `sign(x)` for `|x| > 1`. Bounded, parabolic shoulder.

### 6.2 Bias semantics per mode

The `bias` parameter has slightly different meaning per mode:

- **Tanh, Tube**: pre-shaper DC offset, as today. Generates even harmonics via the asymmetric clip point.
- **Tape**: pre-shaper DC offset (same), but the shape's bounded nature means high bias values saturate one half-cycle to a constant. Useful for compression-with-distortion sound.
- **Diode**: pre-shaper DC offset OR (proposed) asymmetric drive multiplier — positive bias drives `x > 0` harder than `x < 0`. The Pirkle 3/2-power-law character is well-approximated by asymmetric drive even on a cubic shape. To be decided in prototyping; both interpretations are cheap.

### 6.3 Drive mapping rationale

Each mode has a different "drive range that sounds useful" — `tanh` is happy in 1.0..4.0, `cubic` clips hard above 2.0, `asinh` is gentle and wants 1.0..3.0. By normalizing `drive` to 0..1 and mapping per mode, the slider position 0.5 means roughly the same perceptual intensity across modes. This is a UX improvement: users don't need to learn that "drive 4.0 on diode mode is wildly different from drive 4.0 on tube mode."

## 7. Anti-aliasing via ADAA

### 7.1 First-order ADAA, generalized

Given a memoryless nonlinearity `f(x)` with first antiderivative `F(x)` (i.e. `F'(x) = f(x)`), the first-order ADAA output is:

```
            F(x[n]) - F(x[n-1])
y[n]  =  ─────────────────────
              x[n] - x[n-1]
```

When `|x[n] - x[n-1]| < ε` (ill-conditioned case, denominator approaches zero), fall back to the trivial shaper evaluated at the midpoint:

```
y[n]  =  f((x[n] + x[n-1]) / 2)
```

`ε` is typically 1e-5 to 1e-3 depending on precision. We use `ε = 1e-6` with double-precision internal compute.

This introduces a **half-sample group delay**. Not a per-block delay; just a phase shift. Inaudible. No alignment work needed because the wet path is mixed with the dry path through `mix`; for `mix < 1.0` you have a slightly out-of-phase wet against dry, which would matter for nulling tests but not for hearing. We document this and move on.

### 7.2 Antiderivatives per mode

This is the table the implementation needs:

| Mode | `f(x)` | First antiderivative `F(x)` |
|------|--------|---------------------------|
| Tanh | `tanh(x)` | `log(cosh(x))` |
| Tube | `asinh(x)` | `x · asinh(x) - sqrt(1 + x²)` |
| Tape (soft-quad) | piecewise: `sign(x)(1-(1-|x|)²)` for `|x|≤1`, `sign(x)` for `|x|>1` | piecewise polynomial; see Faust `aanl.lib` for the closed form |
| Diode (cubic-clip) | piecewise: `x - x³/3` for `|x|<1`, `sign(x)·(2/3)` for `|x|≥1` | piecewise: `x²/2 - x⁴/12` inside, `sign(x)·(2/3)·x - 1/4` outside |

Closed-form antiderivatives exist for all four. No special functions needed beyond `log`, `cosh`, `asinh`, `sqrt` (all in `<cmath>`). The transcendentals (Tanh, Tube modes) are the expensive ones; Tape and Diode are pure polynomial work and will be the cheapest modes to run.

### 7.3 Low-drive bypass / crossfade

Per the Faust `aanl.lib` documentation note: at low drive (where harmonics generated barely exceed the noise floor) ADAA can introduce *more* noise than it removes. We crossfade between trivial shaper and ADAA shaper based on a function of `drive`:

- `drive < 0.1` (normalized): use trivial shaper (no aliasing risk; cheaper)
- `drive 0.1..0.3`: linear crossfade from trivial to ADAA
- `drive > 0.3`: pure ADAA

This is fully deterministic and adds maybe 5 cycles to the per-sample cost when in the crossfade region. The `mix == 0` early-out remains the dominant optimization for buses with the effect installed but turned off.

## 8. Auto-compensation (output level invariance)

A core UX problem with saturation is that turning up `drive` makes things louder and ALSO changes their character, so A/B-ing drive amounts is confounded by loudness changes. The fix is to subtract the expected loudness gain from the wet path before mixing.

For each mode, the "loudness gain" of the shaper applied to a unit-RMS sine input is computable analytically (or measurable offline). We bake a small table:

```cpp
// For each mode, [drive=0.0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0] → wet RMS / dry RMS
struct ModeCompensation {
    float drive_breakpoints[5];
    float wet_rms_per_dry_rms[5];
};
```

Apply `1.0 / wet_rms_per_dry_rms` (linearly interpolated against current drive) to the wet path. After this, drive sweeps are perceived as character changes, not volume changes. The user's `output_gain` parameter then becomes a pure post-trim with default 1.0 and no need to dial it in by ear.

The table is generated offline by running each mode on a unit-amplitude pink-noise sample at each drive breakpoint, measuring RMS, and computing the ratio. Fixed at build time, no runtime tracking needed.

## 9. Tone / tilt filter

One-pole high shelf with cutoff at ~1 kHz. Gain in dB = `tone * 6.0`:

- `tone = 0`: bypass entirely (filter pointers null, no work). Most common case; should be the default fast path.
- `tone = +1`: pre-emphasis boosts highs by +6 dB before shaper; de-emphasis cuts highs by -6 dB after. Net tonal balance unchanged on dry-equivalent material, but high-frequency content drives the shaper harder → "brighter" saturation, more transient clipping.
- `tone = -1`: opposite — pre cuts highs, de boosts highs. Lows drive the shaper harder → "darker" saturation, more body emphasis.

Implementation is two coupled biquads (or two one-pole shelves) where the de-emphasis filter is the algebraic inverse of the pre-emphasis filter. Standard cookbook. Coefficients computed at `Prepare` from sample rate; updated on `OnParameter` when `tone` changes (rare, not a hot path).

Filter state is per-channel, two floats per filter, four total. Stateless except for the one-pole z-1.

## 10. Validation

Three test layers, modeled on the reverb test structure:

### 10.1 DSP impulse response (per-mode)
For each mode, feed a unit impulse, FFT the output, assert harmonic content matches expected per-mode signature (tanh = odd-dominant, tube = unbounded gentle, tape = even+odd mix, diode = hard shoulder). Tolerances tight enough to catch coefficient regressions but loose enough not to break on minor numerics.

### 10.2 Aliasing test
Feed a 19 kHz sine into the shaper at 48 kHz sample rate at drive=1.0 (highest setting). Run FFT. Assert that energy in the band [20kHz, 24kHz] (which would be where aliasing folds back from the 38kHz second harmonic) is below -80 dBFS. Compare with trivial-shaper variant at the same drive — should show ≥40 dB aliasing improvement. This is the test that proves the ADAA actually works.

### 10.3 Auto-compensation loudness test
Generate pink noise, run through each mode at drive=0.0..1.0 in 0.25 steps. Measure output RMS. Assert output RMS is within ±1 dB of input RMS at every drive setting for every mode. This is what makes drive feel like "character" rather than "volume."

### 10.4 Bypass test
With `mix=0`, assert output is bit-identical to input. (Same test we have today; non-negotiable.)

### 10.5 Backward-compatibility test
Load a v0.28.x `config.json` with `drive=2.5`, `damping=0.4`, `mix=0.2`. Run through the new code path. Assert the output's character is "close enough" to the v0.28.x output of the same config — defined as RMS within ±2 dB and spectral centroid within ±10%. Not bit-identical; we're explicitly changing the shape semantics. But auditioning users shouldn't hear a dramatic difference on existing presets.

## 11. Cost / performance

Rough per-sample cost estimates (cycles, x86-64 modern, single channel):

| Component | Cost when active | Cost when bypassed |
|-----------|------------------|--------------------|
| `mix == 0` early out | 0 | 0 (trivial) |
| Pre-emphasis (`tone == 0`) | ~6 cyc (one-pole) | 0 |
| Drive multiply + bias | ~2 cyc | 0 |
| ADAA wrapper (any mode) | ~8 cyc (compare, branch, divide) | 0 |
| Mode shaper — Tanh | ~25 cyc (log, cosh) | n/a |
| Mode shaper — Tube | ~30 cyc (asinh, sqrt) | n/a |
| Mode shaper — Tape | ~10 cyc (poly) | n/a |
| Mode shaper — Diode | ~8 cyc (poly + branch) | n/a |
| DC blocker | ~6 cyc | 0 |
| Auto-comp lookup | ~5 cyc | n/a |
| De-emphasis | ~6 cyc | 0 |
| Output gain + mix | ~4 cyc | 0 |

**Worst case (Tube mode, tone≠0, mix>0):** ~67 cyc per sample per channel. At 48kHz stereo that's 6.4M cyc/sec, well under 1% of a modern core. Comfortable.

**Current implementation (single tanh, mix>0):** ~25 cyc per sample per channel. So we're 2.5-3× the per-sample cost of the current shaper — but only when the effect is actually doing work. The `mix == 0` early-out remains identical.

## 12. Implementation phasing

The same incremental approach we used for reverb (avoid a single mega-release, ship a series of self-contained improvements):

### Phase 1: ADAA on existing tanh (v0.30.0)
- Add first-order ADAA wrapper around the existing `tanh` shaper
- Keep all four current params unchanged (drive, mix, output_gain, bias)
- No JSON migration, no dock changes
- One unit test: aliasing assertion
- **Biggest acoustic win for the smallest API surface change.** Worth shipping alone.

### Phase 2: Mode selector + four shapes (v0.30.1)
- Add `Saturation_Mode` parameter (ID 26)
- Implement the four shape modes (Tanh, Tube, Tape, Diode) with their ADAA antiderivatives
- Dock support for the discrete mode selector (dropdown widget)
- Drive normalization + soft migration of legacy drive values
- Material preset table is intentionally NOT included in this phase — defer to Phase 4 if useful

### Phase 3: Auto-compensation + DC blocker + param smoothing (v0.30.2)
- Replace static DC subtraction with proper one-pole DC blocker (handles automated bias)
- Build the per-mode compensation table offline; bake into code
- Apply auto-compensation on the wet path
- Add one-pole smoothers on drive/mix/bias to fix zipper noise on automation
- `output_gain` becomes pure post-trim; bound 0..2

### Phase 4: Tone filter (v0.30.3)
- Add `Saturation_Tone` parameter (ID 27)
- Implement the pre/de-emphasis high-shelf pair
- Update dock layout to include tone slider
- Optional: material preset table for environmental saturation presets ("radio_comms", "broken_speaker", "tube_warmth", "tape_glue"). Probably overkill for now; revisit when there's actual demand from sound design.

Phases 1-4 are independent — each is a useful release on its own. Phase 1 is the highest priority by far (audible aliasing is a real problem; the rest are improvements).

## 13. Open questions

1. **Mode 3 (Diode) bias semantics**: pre-shaper DC offset (matches modes 0-2) vs asymmetric drive multiplier (better tube-asymmetry character)? Probably the asymmetric drive interpretation wins on character — needs prototyping to confirm it doesn't sound weird.

2. **Crossfade region for low-drive bypass**: `drive < 0.1` → trivial, `drive > 0.3` → pure ADAA, with a linear crossfade in between. Are those breakpoints right? Should they be per-mode? Probably yes for the more aggressive Diode/Tape modes that benefit from ADAA at lower drive.

3. **Tone filter pivot frequency**: 1 kHz is the textbook choice for "tilt" filters. Should it be a parameter? Almost certainly not — adds a knob that 99% of users won't touch. 1 kHz fixed.

4. **Should we add a `Saturation_Material` lookup table** (similar to `ReverbPresetByMaterial`)? Use cases would be different — saturation isn't a function of environmental material so much as of intent ("make this sound like a tube preamp", "make this sound like a damaged speaker"). Might fit as preset bundles rather than a material lookup. Deferred to Phase 4 or later.

5. **Per-channel vs shared state**: ADAA's `x[n-1]` is per-channel for proper stereo independence. Trivial. But for the auto-compensation gain, should it track per-channel or shared (mono-sum)? Probably shared — saves work and is what most analog units do.

6. **Should "Sat" presets ship in `assets/presets/saturation/*.json`** the way Wwise-style middleware ships sound designer presets? Adjacent question to the material table. Defer.

## 15. Out-of-scope (for awareness, not commitments)

- **Stateful character processing**: dynamic envelope-following saturation, "more drive on transients" — adds complexity, latency, and tuning surface. Probably a separate effect type if ever wanted.
- **IR-based amp/speaker emulation**: convolution box. Different problem entirely.
- **Stereo-link / mid-side processing**: could be added later as a per-effect option, similar to how the compressor will eventually get sidechain link. Not part of this design.
- **Oversampling**: 2× polyphase oversampling is an alternative to ADAA. We choose ADAA on cost grounds. If profiling later shows the ADAA shaper is the hot path on low-end hardware, oversampling is a known fallback (factor of ~2-3× more expensive but more robust at low drive).
